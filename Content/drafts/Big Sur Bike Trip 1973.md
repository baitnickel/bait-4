## Day 1 - July 29

![][map]Peter and I rode with Mom and Frank from Big Sur down to San Luis Obispo, arriving at the Olive house at about 12:30, and found that Paul was not there. Paul had gone to purchase bike racks for Peter and himself to use for carrying sleeping bags. I had been very conscious of the time, even before we arrived at the Olive house, because I was unsure about how far we would be able to ride on this first day, and I had hoped that we would be able to make it as far as San Simon. As we waited for Paul, I began to wonder whether we would have enough time.

Paul arrived before too long, but it took a good deal of time for all three of us to get organized to get our bikes ready to leave, particularly with the trouble that Paul experienced in fitting his bike rack onto his bike. 

Finally, at 3:15, we started away from the Olive house and only went around the corner to a gas station before we stopped again. At the gas station, all of us filled our tires with air and then we were off.

We were already on Highway 1, going through San Luis Obispo, and encountered two of the most difficult hills of the early trip in San Luis Obispo, or just on the outskirts of San Luis Obispo. I was trailing Peter and Paul and assumed at the time that I might be the slowest one on the entire trip. Although this early riding was not too easy, I was able to ride and not walk the bike the whole time. When I caught up with Peter and Paul on flatter ground, Paul pointed out a few places such as Stenner Glen and the college, some distance off the road. Throughout the early going, it was quite obvious that Paul was very familiar with the area that we were riding in.

It was only shortly into the trip that Paul pointed out a good use for the walkie-talkies by indicating that if the leader carried one and the person furthest back carried one, there would be no problem in losing the person in back if he should have bike trouble. Whenever the person in back could not be seen, the leader would take out his walkie-talkie and wait for him to call.

![][aw-morro-bay]At 4:35, the three of us stopped at Safeway in Morro Bay where we bought some food for that night's meal and some spoons and cups. It was here that we encountered the first of a number of people who approached us wondering where we were from and where we were going. This was a young guy with short, dark hair, who rode up to us on a bicycle and invited us to stop at his commune if we'd like something to eat. Paul asked him where it was and he said that it was south of where we were talking to him, and primarily because of that, Paul declined the invitation. We then rode a short distance to an A&W Root Beer stand, where each of us had small lunches and were quickly on our way.

By the time we left Morro Bay it was 5:25, and I had, at this time—and as I had earlier—serious doubts about whether we would be able to make our goal of the day, San Simeon. Peter and Paul, however, were becoming increasingly confident that we would be able to make it to San Simeon before it got dark. Peter had only become convinced while we were in Morro Bay.

![][day-1-rest-stop]Immediately after leaving Morro Bay we returned to Highway 1 and rode through rather isolated country on roads which were rather difficult in some places. By this time, I had already realized that the mental attitude during the bike ride was as important as physical conditioning, and I was trying very carefully to pace myself and to think positively about bike riding.

We passed through Cayucos without too much interest, and we were forced to leave the Highway 1 as it turned into freeway. It was shortly after we had passed through Cayucos when I passed Peter riding up a hill and after turning a corner he called out to me and had me stop, explaining that he was very worn out. Paul had turned a corner and left our sight by this time, so I took the walkie-talkie from my rucksack and gave it to Peter and said, "You want to call him?" So Peter turned it on and tried for a few minutes to get in touch with him before Paul finally answered. Paul said that the place where he was was as good a place as any to take a rest and he would wait there for us to catch up with him. I suggested to Peter that he and I start walking, and we did so, maintaining radio contact with Paul until he was well within our sight.

![][cambria]It was 6:24 when we made this radio contact, and 6:45 before we left this, our first real rest stop, and when we began riding again I took the lead and in perhaps my best riding of the trip, I kept a very constant cadence until 7:45 when I stopped just two miles outside of Cambria to wait for Paul and Peter who were some distance behind me. When they arrived, I was pleased to hear that they had consider the past hour's riding a bit difficult because for me, because I had been concentrating on mental attitude, it had been a fairly easy ride.

At this point we were edging toward San Simeon but it was getting dark as well, and there was some question as to whether we'd be able to make it. Peter, in particular, was rather tired from the day's riding. We had one alternative to San Simeon which was a state beach called San Simeon Beach, which was some seven or eight miles below the city of San Simeon, and we decided to ride at least that far, and see if we could ride further once we got there. When we got to this first state beach, it was already pretty dark, but Paul and I waited for Peter's okay, and when he gave it we moved on for what we thought would be several more miles. But it turned out to be only over a small hill, a total distance of about half a mile at most, and we then came to the Cavalier Inn, where we knew that Mom and Frank were staying for the night. Peter and I talked as Paul rode up in front of us about whether we should stay in the motel for the night instead of camping out as we had planned, and we decided it would be alright as long as we could get up early enough in the morning and get an early start. So we rode up to Paul and told him of our decision, and then rode into the Cavalier Inn, riding around the parking lot for a few moments looking for Mom's white Audi. When we finally found it, it gave us little indication of which motel room was hers. So Peter went and talked to the motel manager and asked which room Mrs. Dickinson was in, and after the motel manager called Mom to ask if it would be alright to give the room number, we learned the room number, and even before Peter got back to the room, Mom had come out of the motel room and seen Paul and I waiting down below.

We ate in a nearby restaurant, each of us having more to drink than to eat, and Paul and I slept out on the balcony of the motel room in our sleeping bags, facing the ocean, with Peter on the floor of the motel room inside in his sleeping bag.

## Day 2 - July 30

We awoke not terribly early after watching some Watergate on television. We went across the street to a pancake restaurant where, after a long wait for a table, we had breakfast. We encountered one man here who was interested in our bike riding and owned a bike store in the Los Angeles area, and he talked to Paul about the best way to carry the sleeping bags and packs, et cetera. Paul told him that the more you could put on the bike the better, and the man mentioned that he had heard different stories about whether to carry the load on your back or on the bike. But Paul insisted that carrying the load on the bicycle was the best idea.

![][dan-san-simeon] ![][pete-san-simeon] ![][paul-san-simeon] 

We left the pancake restaurant at 10:00 and rode for the first part of the trip across rather flat and wide open spaces. We stopped once or twice in these flat areas where Peter took some photographs with the telephoto lens, and where we generally emphasized taking it easy for a few minutes after we had eaten. 

![][ragged-point]The ride continued to be fairly easy until we came to the top of San Luis Obispo county, and Ragged Point, in particular, where we began to enter the most famous section of Highway 1, along the Big Sur area. We passed Ragged Point at 12:25 and for the next few hours we were in for the most difficult bike riding of the entire trip. This riding consisted of long uphill climbs and equally long downhill coasting which, of course, passed much quicker in time than the climbing, and it was simply long stretches of up and long stretches of down, the down never quite as satisfying as we would have liked it to be. It seemed to me a very frustrating experience of having to climb to a certain height and then roll down to the bottom of the hill just so you could climb again. And several hours of this wore me out completely. And I was very disheartened at this point and did a considerable amount of walking my bike.

![][tough-stretch-below-gorda] ![][dan-below-gorda]

Peter and Paul—Paul especially—had a less difficult time, and I encountered them seldom during this period of the ride, the only times being when they stopped to take a rest and wait for me. I was having such a difficult time that, at times, it was even difficult to walk my bike up the hills, even though for Paul it was not so much trouble to keep riding.

We arrived in the small town of Gorda at 3:15, and it appeared to us that the entire town consisted of a gas station, a grocery store, and a café. We encountered one bicyclist who was heading the same direction as we were, who was having apparently nearly as difficult a time as we were. He had stopped at Gorda for the same reason as we did, to eat in the café. We ate not too large a meal in Gorda, knowing that we had difficult rides ahead of us, and savored the drinks that we got here. After eating, we bought some food and candy at the grocery store which adjoined the café, and were ready to leave. The café and grocery store in Gorda were unusual in that it seemed that for every bare space of wall they had put up a sign saying one thing or another about "no bare feet" or "leave packs outside" or "there's a charge for the use of the bathroom if you aren't eating at the café", et cetera, et cetera, and perhaps being slightly paranoid, I noticed a few strange stares at the three of us as we were in Gorda.

![][pacific-valley]We left Gorda at 4:00 and began riding on what pleasantly turned out to be a less difficult ride than we had expected after our previous difficult riding. And at 4:40, we arrived in Pacific Valley, a town very much like Gorda, which didn't seem to consist of anything but a store, gas station, and restaurant. Each of us made use of the bathroom in Pacific Valley and after noting the rock with the name "Dan Mason" on it and a lizard crawling across it, we were on our way again.

The ride north of Pacific Valley was not terribly difficult, but there were moments when I, who was behind Peter and Paul again, was forced to walk my bike up some of the hills. At one point, about a mile below Lucia, I decided for the first time on the bike trip to shift down into my lowest gear—first gear—which I had avoided previously as it seemed to me to be a type of psychological surrender. After I shifted into first gear I felt as though I had given up and would usually have to walk my bike. Anyhow, so I normally avoided shifting into first, and got off my bike when it got difficult in second gear. But, unfortunately, as it turned out, I decided to shift into first gear and as I did so the rear derailleur cable broke, and after a minute or so the seriousness of this problem sunk into my head as I realized that now I had nothing but eighth and tenth gears to get me through some twenty or thirty miles of this kind of road before we could get to a bike store.

I walked my bike for a few moments up the hill and then pulled out the walkie talkie, and began calling Peter or Paul. After a few moments one of them answered and told me that Paul had just had a flat tire. And so the first two bicycle problems that we had on the whole trip occurred within a few minutes of each other. Either Peter or Paul told me on the radio that it was mostly downhill to Lucia, so I got on my bike and coasted to meet them.

![][lucia-sign]When I arrived there, I found that the two of them were sitting on the edge of a grocery store and gas station parking lot in Lucia, where Paul was fixing his flat tire. And so I joined them and began to work on my cable. It was 6:00 as we worked together on our bicycles, my cable having broken at 5:50 and Paul's tire going flat at about 5:55. And Peter, having no work to do on his bike, had been walking around looking at things, and came over to tell us that there was a sign on the grocery store door saying "NO HIPPIES ALLOWED", which struck Paul and I pretty funny. In a few moments, a young man came out and asked us to move all our bicycles and tools into one parking space, rather than being spread out, so that parking spaces would be available for cars. At this time, possibly one or two of the ten or so parking spaces were being used and we thought it was a bit ridiculous, but complied with his request. Only a few moments later, an older man came out and just very bluntly told us to pick up our tools and move down the road, and I think we all immediately assumed that this was the owner of the store and the same person who had put up the "NO HIPPIES ALLOWED" sign. We were all rather astounded after coming face to face with what we termed a "redneck", and we all seemed to be somewhat surprised that such people really did exist.

It was 7:00 before we were able to leave Lucia, after I had used some nylon cord to tie my derailleur, as best I could, into fourth and seventh gear, a medium range which I felt would be the best since I was restricted to only two gears. We rode for a short while before Paul suggested that he and I trade bicycles ’cause he was in the best shape of the three of us, and he felt he didn't need anything lower than fourth gear for most of these hills. So we traded bicycles and we began riding again.

After riding in my most common position in the back of the three of us for some time, I passed Peter at one point who was having a great deal of difficulty, and when I stopped later and waited for him he told me when he caught up with me that he was not feeling very well, and at some point he was even feeling dizzy. So we stopped and rested a while. There was some conjecturing as to how far it was to Big Sur which was our objective for that night, and we could see a sign up ahead  which had the mileage for Big Sur on it, but we weren't close enough to read the mileage. As it turned out, the sign said fifteen miles, and as it was already getting dark, we were forced to change our objective for the night to Julia Pfeiffer Burns State Park, a day-use only camp, which, after stopping again, when Peter and I caught up with Paul to eat a box of raisins for nourishment, we rode on until we got to Julia Pfeiffer Burns at 9:00. Again, we were forced to do some riding in the dark and ate our first meal that we cooked ourselves in a deserted Julia Pfeiffer Burns park, and we went to bed as soon as we finished eating.

## Day 3 - July 31

Paul awoke first at about 6:00. I awoke shortly thereafter, and Peter shortly thereafter. We were up at 6:55 and gathered things together and left camp at 7:15. We had decided that we would ride to Big Sur, which was eleven miles away, and eat breakfast in the lodge there. Peter had predicted it would be about an hour and a half ride, and his prediction was very close, as we went through somewhat difficult riding in very thick fog, so thick that a number of times Paul, who was riding in front of me, would ride into the fog and slowly vanish, and when coasting downhill we all noticed that beads of water would collect in our hair. Peter had taken the lead in this morning's riding to the extent that we didn't see him from the time we left until we got to Big Sur, where he was waiting for us. We passed one girl with two dogs on this morning's riding who stood rather mysteriously in the fog and just as mysteriously gave a simple warning to Paul and I to be careful. As we passed Nepenthe at 8:40 we encountered a few more people who were hitchhiking and had nothing to say. Shortly after Nepenthe, we reached the top of the hill, from where we coasted into Big Sur for several miles, arriving at 9:00.

We ate at the lodge as planned, Peter and I keeping an eye on our favorite waitress there. After eating, we walked our bikes up to the cabins where we sat by the pool for quite a while, just relaxing and looking at things. And after Paul played the piano a little bit, we went down to the Big Sur Lodge grocery store where we got some canned fruit and candy, and finally left Big Sur at noon.

The first part of the riding after Big Sur was pretty easy, going through the town of Big Sur, Paul and I stopping once at a gas station to fill the tires. Peter, once again, had taken off in front of us and out of our sight, but we caught up with him after we had gone through the town of Big Sur and were getting out where we could see the ocean once again. This was the part of the bike ride which I expected to be very very difficult, rivaling the stretch of Highway 1 south of Gorda, and fortunately it did not turn out to be quite so difficult. There was some climbing to do but there was not as much up and down as it was mainly one climb to the top and then a coast down.

At one point riding along this stretch of road where the road hugs the mountains, it curves in and out towards the ocean, it became very very windy, so much so that it knocked me off my bicycle at one point, into the hill which we were hugging as we rode, and as the fog came up fairly thick, the wind appeared to be visible as it made waves in the fog. Paul, who was just in front of me, turned to me and mentioned the Yes song, "South Side of the Sky", and the lyrics of that song seemed to fit this experience just perfectly.

I was not at all afraid to walk my bike through some of this area, but was pleasantly surprised to find out that I didn't need to as much as I expected. And when we finally got to the summit we had an enjoyable ride down the other side of the hill, stopping at one point to look over the edge of the famous steel bridge (Bixby Bridge) over the Little Sur River, I believe it's called (actually, Bixby Creek). And after a momentary stop here we continued on until we finally met up with Peter some seven or eight miles below Carmel Highlands. I was greatly anticipating arriving in Carmel Highlands where we would stop for lunch, so much so that when it turned out to be several miles more than what we expected it became very difficult riding for me and I rode through Carmel Highlands behind Peter and Paul and when they took a rest stop I simply got off my bike and sat down rather dejected.

![][lucia-to-carmel] ![][paul-dan-near-carmel]

In a few moments a couple kids rode in the opposite direction past us, telling us there was a store a short ways down and I got on my bike before Peter or Paul and rode down the hill to the Safeway at which we had stopped at other times—on other trips down to Big Sur, in particular—arriving there at 4:02.

After eating satisfying lunches and having quite a bit to drink to cool us off, we found a phone booth at a Standard station where we discovered that there was a bicycle store nearby, just north of us, so we made the short one-block ride to the bike store where Paul bought a new tire and I bought a new cable. We had both of them put on the bikes, I doing mine myself and Paul having the bike store install his. And then after stopping in a gas station to use the restrooms, we were on our way again.

![][near-carmel] ![][paul-safeway]

We had some difficulty riding through Carmel on some unexpected hills there through the city of Carmel, and it was not until 6:30 that we left Carmel. Just outside of Carmel, as we were trying to decide where to go, a van drove up alongside us and asked if we had seen a dog, which they gave us the description of. He had jumped from the back of their car while they stopped at an intersection. We were a little puzzled that they seemed to be looking too far from where the dog had run away, but they went on, calling out the dog's name.

Paul had decided at this point, although we had originally hoped to ride up through Fort Ord and up toward Watsonville on this day, that we should stop in or around Monterey at, particularly, one of two state beaches—Asilomar State Beach or Monterey State Beach—and camp there for the night while it was still light. So we left Highway 1 and rode out towards the beach on a protruding section of Monterey, and after looking at the first beach, Asilomar State Beach, and riding past it and seeing signs that said "No Fires" and "No Picnicking", we decided that the beach didn't look like a good idea. They were very rocky beaches and at this time there was a number of people still wandering around on them and it didn't look like a good place to stop and cook a meal and bed down for the night. It was getting later and it was beginning to get dark again, and we had no indication from the map of any other place to stay.

So we began to discuss, as we sat outside a motel, whether we should stay in a motel for the night. And as we were deciding how much money we had and whether we could afford to do that and still make it home on the money we had, a girl ran over to us from out of nowhere—a very energetic, red-haired girl who was soon accompanied by a boyfriend, apparently. And he was initially interested in where we were from and headed, and indicated that he had done some bicycling himself, and after asking "What are you doing now?", and we explained that we were trying to figure out where to stay for the night, he told us of a campground in Monterey at the top of a hill (Veteran's Memorial Park), and gave us instructions on our notepaper as to how to get there. She put in a pitch for the restaurant in which she worked before she left us, telling us to stop by at Alice's Restaurant if we had a chance, and we were then on our way following their directions. And without any trouble we found the campground, although I had some difficulty in climbing to the top of the hill on which it was. I resorted to walking my bike most of the way and eventually got there.

Once we found a campsite, a park official came by and talked to Peter and Paul while I was off looking around for alternative campsites, and he told Peter and Paul that since they had ridden up the hill to the campsite on bicycles, that we deserved something, so he wouldn't charge us the dollar-fifty for the night.

We were unable to find any wood at all anywhere in the park, and fortunately were able to use the still-burning coals of an abandoned campsite across from us where we heated up our food, and after it was hot we brought it back to our camp and ate there, once again in the dark, and got to bed shortly thereafter.

## Day 4 - August 1

![][veterans-memorial] ![][alices-restaurant]

It was not until 9:00 when we were able to get up and start leaving the campsite in Monterey, and because we were so late we were forced to pay $1.50 by a park official who walked around in the morning collecting the fees, apparently a different park official than the one we had talked to the night before.

We rode around downtown Monterey until we found a spot which we decided would be a good place to eat and had breakfast here, bought a newspaper, and while Peter and I read the newspaper outside the restaurant, Paul went for a walk around Monterey. We then needed to go to a gas station and look at some maps to see if we couldn't find an alternative to taking the highway all the way around Ford Ord rather than riding through it. Our problem was that the only road going through it that we could see on our maps was freeway. Although Peter and Paul were willing to go around it, by way of Salinas, I was insistent upon finding a way to go through and save a lot of time and trouble.

As it turned out, when we got to the section of Highway 1 that becomes freeway going through Fort Ord, we found that there were bike paths along the freeway, and we had a very pleasant ride along them, very different from what we had experienced the past few days in having to ride dangerously close to cars. We arrived in the town of Marina just on the other side of Fort Ord at 12:35, and without stopping continued on Highway 1 to Castroville.

Just after we passed through Castroville, as we rode along a road bypassing a section of freeway, we encountered a Hells Angel-type motorcyclist who was working on his bike and asked Paul if he could borrow a screwdriver. Paul lent him a screwdriver and Peter followed Paul in stopping there and I followed Peter, and Peter appeared to me to be extremely nervous and upset the whole time that we were there, and I subsequently discovered that he was worried about the motorcyclist because he had borrowed our screwdriver and my knife which has a screwdriver in it and Peter feared for our lives and property and suggested that we simply get on our bikes and leave, even at the risk of leaving our tools with him. But I was reluctant to believe there was any real danger, and Paul was completely trusting of the motorcyclist. So we waited until he had fixed his bike as best he could and gave us back our tools, and after he asked us to get in touch with his girlfriend in Watsonville we began riding on our way again.

![][salinas-road]It was 1:53 when we left the motorcyclist, and we rode along Highway 1 as far as we could until, once again, it became freeway and we had to leave it at Salinas Road where we stopped and made a rest stop. We were not a long time at Salinas Road and immediately after we began riding again, a car with four or five people in it and a young driver wearing a cowboy hat drove by us in the same direction that we were moving and after passing us made a U-turn, stopped on the opposite side of the road, and as we rode by said in an almost conversational tone, "You ugly," to each of us as we passed, and as soon as we had passed, with Paul in the lead, myself in the middle, and Peter behind, the car made another U-turn and started coming back again towards us. Peter commented that the car was coming back toward us, giving us warning of this fact, and when the car got to us, Peter rode off the road and the car swerved and came very close to all of us and was gone, never to be seen again. This occurred at 3:00, and it was ten minutes later, at 3:10, when we arrived in Watsonville. The whole town appeared to us—Paul especially—to be very much like this one cowboy that we ran into, so we didn't even bother to stop here for lunch and just continued on through Watsonville.

When the road on which we traveled finally joined Highway 1 again, it was freeway, but because it was only going to be freeway for another mile or so, we decided to go on it anyway since there were no easy alternatives. We rode along Highway 1 as far as we could just until it became freeway again when we took a rode around the freeway which crossed the freeway and eventually ran into Soquel Road, a frontage road to the freeway which took us into Aptos, eventually.

Soquel Road was a very nice road to ride along. It was fairly flat and quiet, scenic, not too much traffic—one of the more enjoyable roads of the trip. It took us straight into Aptos at 4:30 where we stopped and had lunch in a McDonald's-type store. As we did a lot of times when we stopped and ate places, we had more to drink than we did to eat, and at 5:26 we left Aptos, rode along Soquel Road, passing through the town of Soquel at 5:42, and finally arriving in Santa Cruz, which struck me as a very nice city, at 5:58.

We stopped at Lucky's here and took turns going in the supermarket and watching the bicycles, and picking out the night's dinners as well as the next day's breakfasts. Then we stopped at a gas station to find out how to get onto Highway 9, which would take us up to our objective for the night of Henry Cowell State Park.

Highway 9 was a pretty easy road to ride on initially. There were only a couple miles of fairly difficult riding along Highway 9 before we got to Henry Cowell. The only problems which we found were that the entrance to Henry Cowell was difficult to get to, the way we approached it. We had to go around the top of the park and back down to the other side of the park, so that it was a longer ride and, in some cases, extremely steep ride—a much steeper ride than we'd expected.

We went through Felton at the top of the park at 7:30, and arrived at Henry Cowell at about 8:00. As we went into the entrance, the contact station was unoccupied but a sign said "Camp Full". We waited a minute to see if someone was going to show up at the station, as there were some people next to it working on a truck of some sort. As we waited we noticed, in particular, a patch of poison oak which they had put next to the contact station so that people could recognize it. When no one entered the station, we got on our bikes and rode into the park until we found a campsite which we liked at 8:05 and which was very much empty despite the "Camp Full" sign outside the park.

For the first time, we ate our dinner in daylight—part of our dinner, at least. It was shortly thereafter that we went to bed. We were impressed that night with the fact that we had finally made it back to the Bay Area, and we did quite a bit of talking while we were in our sleeping bags before we actually went to sleep.

## Day 5 - August 2

![][cowell-sleeping] ![][cowell-eating]

We left our campsite after eating what we all agreed was a pretty poor-tasting breakfast of cream of wheat with no milk. We were out of our campsite at 9:55. We rode down a steep hill into Felton, where we arrived at 10:09, and made note of the milage marker so we could estimate how far it would be before we reached the Highway 9 summit above Saratoga.

We went through a number of small towns with Peter and Paul leading the way and me riding behind. Many of the towns were very appealing. We went through Brookdale at 10:40, Boulder Creek at 10:50, making few stops, although we stopped a couple of times to rest. The riding only became difficult after we had gone through Boulder Creek several miles and approached the junction of Highway 9 and Highway 236 which is the highway which goes down into Big Basin.

![][dan-highway9]Once we reached this junction we were in for six miles of fairly difficult riding. Peter and Paul were pretty much together up ahead of me, but I did not see them on the entire ride up along this hill. I did as little walking as possible for the first four miles, or so, and then walked a little over a mile just before I reached the summit as I was beginning to be very tired and especially my back was sore. We had problems of heat, also, on this section that we had not encountered earlier in the trip as we had otherwise been close to the ocean but here were in a very warm spot. And along with the heat were a lot of flies and bugs that were very annoying while you were trying to concentrate on riding.

Eventually, however, I reached the summit at about 2:15. Paul had reached the summit about 1:45 and Peter about fifteen minutes after he did. When I reached the top of the hill, Peter told me that his rear derailleur cable had broken a couple of miles from the top of the hill, and so he had had to do quite a bit of walking himself.

We coasted down the hill some seven miles into Saratoga, where Paul set off in front of Peter and I, who stuck pretty close together, and Peter and I had to stop for some construction near the bottom of the hill. After waiting ten or fifteen minutes or so here we continued to coast down into Saratoga where we stopped at an ice cream store, and after looking for Paul a few moments noticed him up ahead of us, and he turned around and came back and joined us at 2:30. We each had more than one thing at the ice cream store. We then started on our way, leaving Saratoga at 3:09, riding down Saratoga Avenue to Payne, Payne down to Central, and then through the park, across the catwalk, and home at 3:42.
